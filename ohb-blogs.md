Are You a Warrior or a Complainaholic? 

Part 2 

3 Ways to Stop Being a Complainaholic 

In Part 1, we talked about realizing you might be a Complainaholic—someone who vents, blames, and grumbles by default.
Now it’s time to do something about it. 

You don’t need more willpower. You need a better strategy. 

Here are 3 ways to stop being a Complainaholic: 

1. Realize You Have a Problem 

It’s the first step in every recovery program for a reason. You can’t overcome what you won’t admit. 

This applies to more than just complaining. Think about people battling addiction—alcohol, food, even social media. The turning point is always the same: awareness. 

If you’ve caught yourself complaining a lot lately, don’t beat yourself up. Just own it. That awareness is power. 

Try this:
Track how often you complain in one day. Put a rubber band on your wrist and switch it every time you notice yourself grumbling. You’ll be shocked. And more importantly—you’ll be free to change it. 

2. Replace the Habit 

You don’t just break habits—you replace them.
That’s straight from Charles Duhigg’s bestselling book The Power of Habit, where he lays out the “habit loop”: 

  

Cue → Routine → Reward 

Here’s what that looks like in real life: Sports Example 

Cue: You get benched or don’t play much.
 
Routine: You vent to teammates—“Coach doesn’t like me.”
 
Reward: You feel a little better. A few teammates agree with you.
Warrior Shift
 
Cue: You’re benched.
 
New Routine: You ask, “What can I control right now?” You lock in, encourage others,
and stay ready.
 
New Reward: You build trust. You earn respect. You feel proud of how you handled it.
Everyday Life Example
 
Cue: You get a bad grade or tough feedback at work.
 
Routine: You complain to a friend—“That wasn’t fair.”
 
Reward: You get sympathy. You feel justified.
Warrior Shift:
 
Cue: The same frustration.
 
New Routine: You pause and ask, “What can I learn from this?” You make a small plan to
improve.
 
New Reward: You grow. You take back control. You feel momentum.
Ideas for Replacing the Routine:
 
Gratitude Walk (from Jon Gordon): Speak out loud what you’re thankful for while walking.
 
Find the Hearts (Mel Robbins): Train your brain to find hearts in nature or design— builds the habit of looking for the good.
 
Toothbrush Thanks: I’ve started thinking of 3 things I’m thankful for while brushing my teeth. At first, it felt forced. Now, it flows.
 
Journaling: Doesn’t work for me personally, but for many people, writing it down is key.
3. Change Who You Spend Time With
Jim Rohn said it best:
“You are the average of the five people you spend the most time with.”
That applies to mindset, not just money.
 

  

Hang with complainers? You’ll complain.
Hang with competitors? You’ll raise your game. 

Check yourself:
Have you ever caught yourself talking bad about someone, only to realize you were actually avoiding something in your own life? 

Next time that happens, ask:
“What am I covering up by complaining about them?” 

That question might sting. But it leads to something way better than sympathy: it leads to growth. 

Final Thought 

This isn’t about pretending everything’s perfect. It’s about choosing discipline over drama. 

You don’t become a Warrior by chance.
You become one by noticing your patterns, replacing what’s not helping, and surrounding yourself with people who call you higher. 

Gratitude isn’t soft. It’s strategic. And it just might be the most underrated toughness tool you’ve got.

# Are You A Complainaholic? Part 1
Part 1 

What’s a Complainaholic? 

A Complainaholic is someone who complains out of habit—often without realizing it—and gets a temporary high from venting, blaming, or talking about others. It might sound harmless. But over time, it drains your energy, poisons team culture, and weakens your mindset. The worst part? You usually don’t know you’ve become one until someone calls you out... or you finally call yourself out. 

What the Warrior Code Says 

In my book, The 10 Qualities of a Warrior, the number one trait is this: 

“Warriors don’t complain.” Bruce
Lee said it best:
“Warriors do not complain; they adapt themselves to the sum of circumstances which they are placed.” 

I’ve seen this up close. At small schools with little budget, I’ve seen coaches and players dig deep, figure it out, and stay focused on competing—not complaining. At bigger schools with charter buses, Nike gear, and gourmet meals—I’ve seen endless complaints. About playing time. About roommates. About the weather. 

I’ve also met athletes with real adversity—like the two incredible young people I worked with, both born with one arm, who refused to make excuses or feel sorry for themselves. They inspired teammates and built trust by showing up with purpose, not pity. 

Bottom line: complaining is a choice. So is competing. 

3 Reasons Why We Complain 

1. It Feels Good (For a Moment) 

Science shows that complaining gives a brief hit of dopamine—the brain’s reward chemical. That moment of “relief” feels good. But it’s not real progress. It’s just mental junk food. 

Complaints are just excuses dressed up to sound reasonable. “Coach doesn’t like me.”
“It’s hot.” 

  

“I missed a shot because the gym was weird.” None of that makes you better. 

Complaining feels like relief. But it’s actually a retreat. 

2. It’s a Habit 

We complain because we’ve trained ourselves to. It’s our default. 

In his bestselling book The Power of Habit, author Charles Duhigg explains the habit loop like this:
Cue → Routine → Reward
- Something frustrates you (cue) 

- You complain (routine)
- You feel a little better (reward) 

Over time, this loop becomes automatic. But warriors don’t run on feelings—they run on discipline. 

3. It’s the Easy Way Out 

Complaining is easier than growing.
It’s easier to talk about a teammate than talk to them.
It’s easier to blame the coach than outwork the problem. And yes—I’ve been guilty of it too. 

When I was a high school AD, I would send out emails celebrating a team—and immediately get replies complaining about unrelated things. I’d go home frustrated and vent to my wife... And she’d look at me and say, “You’re complaining just like them.” 

Oof. 

This is Step One: Admitting We Have a Problem 

If any of this hits home, good. It means you’re paying attention.
This is Part One of the journey—realizing you’re a Complainaholic. Not forever. But right now. 

Next time, we’ll talk about what to do about it. But for now, just notice. Pay attention to how often it comes up. Track it if you have to. Awareness is the beginning of change.

# Mental Toughness vs Mental Health
Mental Toughness vs. Mental Health: The Strength to Push Through—And the Wisdom to Seek Help 



I believe in mental toughness. It’s what I do for a living—teaching teams, leaders, and individuals how to push through obstacles, develop resilience, and refuse to quit when things get hard. 

And let’s be honest: people need to be tougher. Myself included. We live in a world where too many give up at the first sign of adversity. Grit matters. Perseverance matters. Without them, no one achieves anything worthwhile. 

But here’s the problem: you can be mentally tough without being mentally healthy. 

Pushing through pain, suffering in silence, and pretending everything is fine—that’s not toughness. That’s denial. And denial doesn’t just hurt you; it can cost you everything. 

When Grit Becomes Dangerous 

Mental toughness has been glorified for years. We celebrate the athlete who plays through injury, the executive who burns the midnight oil, and the coach who never lets emotions show. But what happens when toughness becomes a mask? 

I’ll never forget one of my toughest basketball players—an All-American. When he played for me, he was the last person I would have ever expected to struggle with mental health. He was confident, relentless, and mentally tough in every way. But after his playing days were over, he faced battles that no one saw coming. 

The same thing happened to Michael Phelps. The most decorated Olympian of all time— the very definition of grit and perseverance—struggled with depression and suicidal thoughts. He had spent his whole life mastering physical and mental toughness, yet after the 2012 Olympics, he found himself in a dark place, questioning whether life was worth living. 

If someone as mentally tough as Michael Phelps can struggle, what does that tell us? It tells us that toughness alone isn’t enough.
Mental Toughness and Mental Health Are Not Opposites—They Are Partners 

Success isn’t about choosing one or the other; it’s about knowing when to flip the switch. 1. Grit is essential. It pushes us through the hard days, helps us fight for our dreams, and makes sure we don’t quit when things get uncomfortable. 

  

2. But toughness without self-awareness is reckless. The strongest people aren’t the ones who pretend everything is fine—they’re the ones who know when to reach out for help. 

Take Grundy, my rescue Pitbull. He survived alone in the woods for over a year, enduring starvation, predators, and injury. His grit kept him alive. But when we brought him home, he was still trapped in survival mode. Toughness had helped him endure, but healing required something else—trust, care, and support. 

We’re the same way. Grit can get us through the storm, but we can’t thrive if we never let ourselves recover. 

Building a Culture of Mental Toughness AND Mental Health 

So how do we get this right? How do we create a mindset that values toughness while also recognizing when help is needed? 

1. Redefine Strength 

Strength isn’t just pushing through—it’s also knowing when to take a step back and get help. The toughest leaders, athletes, and professionals understand this. 

2. Check on Your “Strong” People 

The ones who seem the toughest are often carrying the heaviest loads. Ask. Listen. Let them know they don’t have to carry it alone. 

3. Encourage Grit—But Not at the Expense of Well-Being 

Great teams, families, and businesses thrive when they expect toughness but also create space for honesty and support. High standards and high empathy can coexist. 

4. Normalize Seeking Help 

Michael Phelps didn’t find healing by ignoring his struggles. He found it by getting help, opening up, and realizing he wasn’t alone. That’s real strength. 

The Real Test of Mental Toughness 

Grit matters. I believe that with all my heart. But grit without mental health is a ticking time bomb. We have to stop acting like toughness and vulnerability are enemies. The greatest athletes, leaders, and warriors in history have all known when to fight through—and when to seek help. 

So ask yourself: 

  

• Where do you need to push harder?
• Where do you need to stop pretending everything is fine and get support? 

Flipping the switch between grit and empathy isn’t weakness. It’s wisdom. And wisdom is what separates those who just survive from those who truly thrive.

# The Anxious Generation: What Coaches and Leaders Can Learn
We are raising kids—and coaching athletes—in a world that looks nothing like the one we grew up in. And we’re just now beginning to understand what it’s costing them.

Jonathan Haidt’s powerful new book, The Anxious Generation, lays it out clearly: the rise in anxiety, depression, and loneliness among young people isn’t random. It started around 2010, the same time smartphones and social media became constant companions. The numbers are staggering:

• Rates of clinical depression among teen girls doubled between 2010 and 2020.

• ER visits for self-harm tripled among preteen girls in the same timeframe.

• Boys are affected too—screen time replaces real-world experiences that are critical for development: unsupervised play, physical movement, eye contact, even boredom.

The result? A generation that is highly connected digitally but deeply disconnected emotionally.

As someone who’s spent years coaching and teaching mental toughness, I’ve had to wrestle with this. I used to think grit alone could carry kids through anything. Push harder. Tough it out. Keep going. And sometimes, that worked.

But not always.

Because grit without emotional support isn’t strength—it’s survival mode.

  

Mental Toughness vs. Mental Health

I’ve seen players fake strength until they broke. I’ve done it myself. I’ve coached All-Americans who looked unstoppable but were quietly falling apart. I’ve also had players—and friends—who didn’t make it through their darkest moments.

We need to stop pretending these are separate conversations.

You can be mentally tough and mentally unhealthy.

You can be the hardest worker and still be hurting.

What Haidt’s book helped me see is that our world has changed—but our response hasn’t. We’re still telling kids to be tougher, when what they really need is someone who knows when to push them and when to ask, “Are you okay?”

That’s where the idea of flipping the switch comes in.

  

Grundy and Sadie: Learning to Flip the Switch

I recently wrote a book called Grundy and Sadie: Unleashing the Power of Grit and Empathy in Life and Leadership. It’s about two dogs we love—and the wildly different lessons they’ve taught me.

Grundy, a rescue Pitbull, survived more than a year alone in the Tennessee woods. His story is all about raw, relentless grit.

Sadie, a Goldendoodle, is pure empathy. She senses pain before it’s spoken and leans in with love.

They couldn’t be more different, and yet they always have each other’s back. Grundy once fought off a German Shepherd to protect Sadie. Sadie later sensed Grundy was having a seizure and cried until I went outside to help him.

They don’t compete.

They complement.

And that’s exactly what young people need from us right now.

What This Means for Coaches, Leaders, and Parents

Haidt calls for what he terms a “return to play-based childhood.” Less phone time. More real-life connection. More freedom, more movement, more eye contact. We don’t need perfection—we need presence.

And we need leaders who know when to flip the switch:

1. Mistakes Are Not Failure

Create space for kids to make mistakes, explore, mess up, and try again. Grit is developed through struggle—real-world struggle, not online comparison.

2. Push Your Athletes—But Check In, Too

Don’t assume the “tough ones” are fine. Ask real questions. Let your team know you see their humanity, not just their hustle.

3. Normalize Asking for Help

I used to think asking for help was weak. Now I know it takes more courage to speak up than to stay silent. That’s the message we need to model.

4. Be the Kind of Leader Who Knows When to Switch Gears

Sometimes you need to be Grundy. Sometimes you need to be Sadie.

Great leadership is knowing when to be which.

  

Final Thought: Strength Isn’t Either/Or

Haidt’s research explains the “why” behind the mental health crisis. My work—and my life—have forced me to wrestle with the “what now?”

The answer isn’t to soften kids, so they won’t struggle.

And it’s not to harden them so they hide their pain.

It’s to build them—fully.

Build their grit.

Build their empathy.

And show them how to live both, one moment at a time.

If Grundy and Sadie can figure it out, maybe we can too.

1. They Don’t Waste Time Feeling Sorry for Themselves: Mentally strong individuals don’t dwell on self-pity. Instead, they focus on solutions and taking positive action.

3. They Don’t Shy Away from Change: Instead of fearing change, they embrace it as an opportunity for growth and development.
4. They Don’t Focus on Things They Can’t Control: They recognize that there are things beyond their control and focus their energy on what they can influence.

7. They Don’t Dwell on the Past: Instead of ruminating on past mistakes or missed opportunities, they learn from them and focus on the present and future.

# EMBRACE PERSONAL RESPONSIBILITY
EMBRACE PERSONAL RESPONSIBILITY

“The warrior does not seek blame for the world’s problems, but neither does he succumb to them. He takes responsibility for his own life and for the world around him.” – Steven Pressfield

I firmly believe that a lack of responsibility among team members can significantly impact a team’s culture and negatively affect its overall success. Warriors believe in taking personal responsibility instead of blaming others for mistakes or failures. They understand that success and failures are a result of a combination of factors, and that everyone has a role to play. Embracing personal responsibility and avoiding blame are crucial for building a positive team culture.

At Lee University, our basketball team had a bittersweet experience when we won a crucial non-conference game on Thursday night, only to suffer an upset loss on Saturday. We had a player miss two free throws late in the game that would have secured the victory. The defeat was not solely due to the player missing a couple of crucial free throws, but rather a culmination of various factors.

Our captain hosted a party after the Thursday night win, which could have contributed to the team’s subpar performance. At the Saturday shootaround, I noticed a lack of focus and energy among the players, including one of our starters who was known for struggling to maintain concentration. When I asked him if he even knew who we were playing, he gave the wrong answer which further confirmed my concerns.

Upon reflection, I realized as the head coach, I was responsible for scheduling a non-conference game so close to a conference game. This impacted the team's ability to fully recover and perform their best. By embracing personal responsibility and taking a step back to examine the situation, I learned from this experience and did a much better of job scheduling in the future.

Another example is when our team had an early morning practice; I had delegated the responsibility of waking me up to my wife, knowing she was an early riser. Unfortunately, she forgot to do so, and I ended up sleeping through the practice. In my frustration, I ended up blaming her and yelling at her. I later realized that it was not her fault but mine. I should have taken the necessary steps to ensure I would not miss practice, such as setting an alarm or having backup plans in place. By placing the blame solely on my wife, I failed to take responsibility for my own actions and overlook the role I played in the situation.

One year, we were fortunate enough to sign a talented player from a junior college where he had an impressive two-year career. However, during his first semester with us, he was struggling to adapt and was not seeing much playing time. As his coach, I was concerned about his confidence and well-being and decided to have a one-on-one meeting with him. To my surprise, he reassured me that he was aware of his current performance and was not discouraged. He promised that once he got more comfortable with our system, he would perform better. Little did I know, our approach to utilizing his skillset was not accurate. We were trying to make him fit into a mold that did not align with his strengths.

Once we changed the way we utilized him, he excelled. It would have been easy for him to place blame on the coaching staff, but he never did. His hard work and determination paid off as he was named the MVP of the conference tournament.

Through a variety of experiences, such as the ones I mentioned, I have come to deeply appreciate the significance of personal accountability. I have learned the importance of taking ownership of my own mistakes and recognizing the need for others to do the same.

# Feed the Positive
“Positive thinking will let you do everything better than negative thinking will.”

- Zig Ziglar



Warriors focus on feeding the positive dog within themselves, as opposed to dwelling on negativity. This concept, from Jon Gordon, states that we all have two internal dogs constantly fighting in our heads, and the one we feed the most is the one that wins. In tough times, warriors strive to find the positives. This certainly applies to how we see people and how it affects our relationships. Do we choose to see someone’s positives or their negatives?

Although I may not always be successful at finding the positive aspects in situations, there is one case where I have been able to do so. I’ve had some serious skin cancer over the course of my life, including one significant surgery. I go to the dermatologist on a regular basis to get spots frozen off and biopsies when needed. This isn’t enjoyable, it hurts, but when it’s happening to me, I think of how much better it is than having to go through chemotherapy. I think of my former friend and boss at Bluefield College who died at age 41 from a rare form of cancer and all the pain he went through as he slowly died.

By maintaining a positive outlook, warriors attract success and longevity into their lives. Studies show that individuals with a positive mindset lead more fulfilling lives and are more successful than those with negative attitudes. Additionally, they are more appealing to others, as positivity enhances relationships and strengthens bonds.

Three practical ways to apply this Quality of a Warrior to your sport:

1.  Practice finding the positive in difficult situations. When something bad happens, try to find the silver lining or an opportunity to learn and grow. This mindset shift can help you stay positive and motivated, even when faced with adversity.

2.  Take a gratitude walk or practice gratitude journaling. Focus on the things in your life that you are thankful for and take time to appreciate them. This can help you stay positive and motivated and improve your overall well- being.

3.  Participate in a sports mission trip to a third-world country. This can be a great way to gain a different perspective on life and sport, and to give back to the community. It can also be a humbling and life-changing experience that can help you appreciate what you have and to be more grateful for it.

The Pinky: (Doesn’t know why they’re there) may not have a clear role or purpose on the team.

The thumb is the most important finger and without it, the hand is not able to function properly. Similarly, a warrior’s leadership, positive attitude, and commitment are essential for a team to function well and achieve its goals. # Warriors Don't Complain
"Warriors do not complain; they adapt themselves to the sum of circumstances which they are placed.” – Bruce Lee

As a warrior, one must refrain from complaining. Complaining depletes energy and dampens team morale. Lou Holtz famously said, “80% of people don’t care about your problems, while the rest are happy you have them.” During my time as an assistant women’s basketball coach at the University of Illinois, I was amazed at all the complaining I heard. Despite the luxurious amenities of charter flights, gourmet restaurants, and top-notch hotels, many of the coaches and athletes still found reasons to complain. This was a stark contrast to my previous experiences as a coach at smaller colleges, where the focus was simply on making do with what resources were available.

On the other hand, during my time as the top assistant at Presbyterian College, I never heard my boss, Dustin Kerns, complain. Despite facing challenges and limitations as the smallest Division 1 school in the country, he would always push us to find solutions and “figure it out.” Complaining does nothing but drain energy and positivity. True warriors do not engage in it.

In warrior culture clinics, I have met some incredible people with amazing stories. I have met two exceptional athletes from two different states, both of whom were born with only one arm. One of them was a young lady from Alabama who played basketball and softball in high school, while the other was a college lacrosse player at Belmont-Abbey College. The incredible courage they exhibit is truly awe-inspiring, and neither of them harbors self-pity.

The young lady, now 19, runs a highly successful clothing business that she started, while the young man plays a vital role on his very successful college lacrosse team. The respect and admiration that their teammates have for them is difficult to put into words and it serves as an inspiration to everyone around them. Despite the challenges they face, they choose not to complain, which is a testament to their strength of character. It was an honor to meet them both and hear their stories.

Overall, avoiding complaining and focusing on personal growth can lead to increased resilience, improved problem-solving skills, greater self-awareness, improved relationships, and increased productivity. It can be an important step towards achieving success and fulfillment in both your personal and professional life.

Please be aware that speaking negatively about a teammate is a type of complaint. Whenever I hear someone speaking negatively about another person, I am always left wondering what they might be saying about me behind my own back. Talking badly about your teammates can have several harmful effects. First, it can create a toxic team culture where trust and camaraderie are diminished, leading to a breakdown in team morale and motivation. Secondly, it can damage the relationships between teammates, leading to a lack of cooperation and synergy on the court/field. Thirdly, it can cause negative feelings and hurt among those who are talked about, leading to a lack of confidence and performance. Ultimately, talking badly about teammates can have serious consequences that not only affect team performance but also the overall well-being of team members.

Three practical ways to apply this Quality of a Warrior to your sport:

1.  Instead of complaining about a problem, focus on finding a solution. This mindset shift can help you take a proactive approach to problem-solving and ultimately lead to better results.

2.  A “complaint fast” is a useful tool to help you break the habit of complaining. By committing to not complaining for a set period of time, you can train yourself to focus on solutions instead of problems.

3.  You are the average of the five people you spend the most time with. Surround yourself with positive, solution-oriented people. Being around others who complain habitually can be draining and may negatively impact your own mindset. Spend time with people who inspire and motivate you and avoid those who bring you down.
